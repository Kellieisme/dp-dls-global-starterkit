Lodge surviving Cape Fiske's cold

A skeptical look at Cape Fiske's newest hotel and its chilly realities

Dr. Elara Voss April 24, 2024

When I first heard about Frosthaven Lodge, perched on the icy edge of Cape Fiske, Antarctica, I imagined a cozy haven amid the endless white—warm fires, plush beds, and gourmet meals served with a side of breathtaking views. What I found, however, was a bit more complicated, and frankly, a little less glamorous than the glossy brochures suggest.

For starters, the hotel rooms, despite their panoramic windows, seem to have been constructed with a surprising lack of insulation. Guests are reportedly bundled up in heavy sleeping bags and forced to wear down pajamas just to survive the night. Luxury, it seems, comes with a side of frostbite risk. One can't help but wonder if the architects were perhaps a bit too optimistic about the warmth their design could provide in a place where the mercury regularly plunges well below freezing.

Access to Frosthaven Lodge is exclusively by ski plane, which sounds adventurous until you learn that the fleet consists of aircraft at least 50 years old, lovingly maintained by a team of Argentinian Air Force pensioners. These seasoned mechanics keep the vintage planes flying, but the thought of relying on half-century-old machines to ferry guests over treacherous ice fields adds a layer of uncertainty to the whole enterprise. It's a bit like booking a luxury cruise on a ship that's seen more battles than calm seas.

One detail that caught my eye upon arrival was a sign posted prominently at the lodge's entryway, proudly displaying the number of days since the last "incident"—a term they use to mean any accident resulting in injury. On the day I toured the facility, the sign read "2 days". Two days without injury might sound reassuring in some contexts, but here, in a place where the environment is as unforgiving as it is beautiful, it felt like a stark reminder of the risks guests willingly take. It made me wonder how often that number resets and what kinds of mishaps are quietly swept under the rug.

Hair fibers reinventing construction

How a hairdresser's discarded clippings became the secret ingredient in bridges that last… well, a little longer

Marcus L. Fenwick March 28, 2024

Who would have thought that the humble hair clipping, usually swept off salon floors and tossed without a second thought, could one day hold the key to revolutionizing construction? Enter Lila Maren, a hairdresser with a flair for invention and a knack for turning the ordinary into the extraordinary. Her creation, "Keratincrete," is a building material made from processed human hair clippings that's stronger than concrete and steel—or at least, that's the claim, with a wink.

Keratincrete's rise to fame began in Maren's bustling salon, where she noticed the mountains of hair clippings piling up daily. Instead of letting them go to waste, she teamed up with some curious materials scientists to see if those strands of keratin could be transformed into something useful. The result? A composite material that's surprisingly tough, lightweight, and eco-friendly. It's made by blending hair fibers with a secret eco-resin, creating panels and beams that can be used in construction.

Now, before you start imagining bridges made entirely of hair, let me clarify: Keratincrete is usually combined with other materials. But it's already been put to the test in several bridge projects, where it's credited with reducing bridge failures by a modest but notable 2%. Even more impressively, it has extended the service life of some bridges by a whopping six minutes. Yes, six whole minutes—enough time to enjoy an extra cup of coffee or finish that podcast episode before the next inspection.

One unexpected side effect of this keratin craze? People are actually getting paid to get their haircuts. Salons participating in the program offer small incentives to customers who donate their clippings, turning what was once a waste product into a valuable resource. So next time you're in the chair, you might just be contributing to the next generation of eco-friendly skyscrapers or bridges—talk about a hair-raising investment!

Keratincrete isn't just strong; it's also fire-resistant, lightweight, and surprisingly insulating. Plus, it's biodegradable, meaning buildings made with it can be recycled or safely broken down at the end of their lifespan. This makes it a darling of the green construction movement, which is always on the lookout for materials that reduce environmental impact.
